Louis Daniel Armstrong

"Charismatic and innovative in the history of jazz"

He is one of the most charismatic and innovative figures in the history of jazz and, probably, its most popular musician. Thanks to his musical abilities and his brilliant personality, he transformed the jazz from its initial status as dance music, into a form of popular art. Although at startup of his career cemented his fame above all as a cornetist and trumpeter, later it would be his condition as a vocalist which would consecrate him as an internationally recognized figure

  • Louis Armstrong was born into a very poor family in one of the slums of New Orleans.
  • Part of his childhood education was obtained by wandering the streets and working as a junk dealer. While still very young, he was able to be aware of the terrible racial hatred that existed in the United States in the first decades of the 20th century
  • In 1914, after his release from reform school, he worked as a coal seller, a milk delivery man, a ship stevedore banana growers and other similar jobs
  • He also began to work in the cabarets of Storyville, where all the nightspots of the city were concentrated; there he meets cornetist Joe King Oliver, who was his mentor
  • Between 1918 and 1919, already with a well-earned reputation as a cornetist, he was hired by the conductor Kid Ory, thanks to a recommendation from Joe King Oliver, who had left the cornetist position.
  • In 1922, Armstrong joined the general exodus of musicians to Chicago, where he had been Invited by Joe King Oliver to join his Creole Jazz Band as second cornetist. the orchestra of Oliver was, in the early twenties, the best and most influential swing group in Chicago
  • In 1923, Armstrong made his debut with the Oliver Orchestra for the Okeh Records label. In their early recordings of him included some solos and asides as the band's second cornet; in 1924, without However, he was already the most important and most creative soloist in the group
  • In February 1924 he married Lillian Hardin, Oliver's pianist, who encouraged him to leave Oliver in order to achieve higher artistic goals. So he parted ways with Oliver amicably and, after briefly working with Ollie Powers, he left for New York
  • His fame quickly reached the ears of the best African-American conductor of the moment, Fletcher Henderson, who offered him a contract to join his band, the Fletcher Henderson Orchestra, the main band African American of the time.
